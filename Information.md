**Member 1: The Classical Modeler (Random Forest Baseline)** This member will build a **Random Forest (RF)** model to capture stable, non-linear market trends. RF is ideal for a hackathon because it requires very little hyperparameter tuning and is highly robust against overfitting.

- **Inputs:** Tabular, single-day features including the OHLCV data, combined with engineered technical indicators like the 50-day Simple Moving Average (SMA), Relative Strength Index (RSI), and MACD.
- **Architecture:** The model is an ensemble of decision trees that uses bagging (bootstrap aggregating) and random feature subsampling to make predictions.
- **Configuration:** Setting the model to use 100 decision tree estimators is a strong and fast baseline for predicting the next-day return or price.

**Member 2: The Deep Learning Modeler (GRU Network)** This member focuses on volatile, sequential patterns using a **Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU)**. GRUs are highly recommended over LSTMs for time-constrained projects because they merge the input and forget gates into a single update gate, making them computationally faster to train while still capturing long-term dependencies in the stock data.

- **Inputs:** A 60-day sliding window of normalized historical stock prices and technical indicators.
- **Layer 1:** A GRU layer with 100 units (set to return the full sequence), followed by a Dropout layer with a 0.20 rate to prevent the model from memorizing noise.
- **Layer 2:** A second GRU layer with 50 units (returning only the final state), followed by another 0.20 Dropout layer.
- **Output Head:** A Dense (fully connected) layer with 25 neurons using a ReLU activation function, feeding into a final Dense output layer with a single linear neuron to predict the exact next-day price.
- **Training:** Compile using the Adam optimizer (0.001 learning rate) and Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss, with a batch size of 32.

**Member 3: The Meta-Learner (XGBoost Ensembler)** This member acts as the bridge, building an **XGBoost** model to dynamically combine the outputs of Member 1 and Member 2 into a single, highly accurate forecast.

- **Inputs:** The input layer for this model consists of the predicted prices generated by the Random Forest and the GRU models, along with recent volatility metrics or technical features.
- **Architecture:** XGBoost is an advanced gradient-boosting framework of regression trees that is highly efficient and resistant to multicollinearity.
- **The Logic:** Instead of just averaging the two predictions, the XGBoost model acts as a dynamic weighting scheme. It learns the market conditions under which the Random Forest is more accurate (stable days) versus when the GRU is more accurate (volatile days), and adjusts its final next-day price prediction accordingly.

Z-score normalization (often called standard scaling) transforms your stock data features so that they have a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one. This process removes differences in scale between features with vastly different ranges—such as price and trading volume—making it much easier for neural networks to detect patterns and converge efficiently during training.

Mathematically, you calculate the Z-score for each data point by subtracting the mean of the feature and dividing by its standard deviation.

For your hackathon, here is the best way to implement it:

1. **Use Scikit-Learn:** Import `StandardScaler` from the `sklearn.preprocessing` module.
2. **Prevent Data Leakage:** This is a critical step where many predictive models fail. You must fit the scaler **only** on your training data. Once the scaler has learned the mean and standard deviation of the training set, you apply that exact same scaler to transform both your training data and your validation/test data. If you fit the scaler on your entire dataset before splitting it, statistical information from the future bleeds into your training process, which will artificially inflate your accuracy and ruin the model's ability to generalize to unseen data.
3. **Watch for Outliers:** Z-score normalization assumes your data distribution is roughly symmetric. If your stock data has extreme outliers (such as massive, irregular volume spikes), Z-scores can be highly sensitive to them. If you notice this negatively impacting your model, you may need to clip the outliers first or consider using a robust scaler that relies on the median instead.

To implement a financial turbulence index to protect your capital, you will use a mathematical formula that measures extreme, unusual asset price movements across your entire basket of stocks. Essentially, it calculates how far today's market behavior deviates from normal historical behavior, factoring in the correlations between your defense stocks and SPY.

Here is the exact formula used in quantitative research to calculate the turbulence index for a given day t:

Turbulencet​=(yt​−μ)Σ−1(yt​−μ)′

Here is what each component means and how your team can calculate it in Python using `pandas` and `numpy`:

- yt​ **(Current Returns):** A vector of the daily returns for all your stocks on the current day t.
- μ **(Historical Average):** A vector of the average daily returns for each stock over a historical rolling window (e.g., the past 60 or 252 trading days).
- Σ−1 **(Inverse Covariance):** The inverse of the historical covariance matrix of your stocks' returns over that same rolling window. You can calculate the covariance matrix using `pandas.DataFrame.cov()` and invert it using `numpy.linalg.inv()`.

How to Implement the Capital Protection Logic

Once Team Member 3 (your meta-learner) calculates this daily turbulence score, you implement it as a hard "kill-switch" rule that overrides your predictive models:

**1. Set a Threshold:** You define a specific turbulence threshold. If you want a highly risk-averse strategy, you set this threshold lower. **2. The Trigger (Market Crash Mode):** If the calculated Turbulencet​ spikes above your threshold, the system flags extreme market conditions. Your algorithm must immediately **halt all buying and sell off all held shares** to liquidate your portfolio into safe cash. **3. The Recovery (Resume Trading):** Your algorithm continues to monitor the market daily but keeps your capital in cash. It only resumes normal trading (listening to the XGBoost, Random Forest, and GRU predictions) once the turbulence index drops back below your safe threshold.

In historical backtests, incorporating this exact turbulence index allowed algorithms to cut catastrophic losses and successfully survive major market crashes, such as the March 2020 COVID-19 crash.
